# Solar 101

Solar energy is the most abundant power source on Earth. Modern solar panels capture a portion of that sunlight and convert it into usable electricity for your home.

## How It Works

1. **Sunlight hits the solar cells** inside your panels.
2. **Photons excite electrons**, creating direct current (DC) electricity.
3. **An inverter converts DC to AC** — the alternating current your home runs on.
4. **Electricity is used, stored, or sent to the grid** depending on your setup.

## System Components

**Solar Panels**
Each panel contains layers of silicon-based solar cells. When sunlight hits them, the photovoltaic effect creates an electrical current. Typical panel: ~4ft × 6ft, ~30 lbs. Panels are grouped into an array.

**Inverters**
Convert DC from the panels into AC for your home. Three types:
- **String inverters** — one central unit, cost-effective, output affected by shading
- **Microinverters** — one per panel, best performance, higher cost
- **Power optimizers** — hybrid, panel-level conditioning with a central inverter

**Mounting & Racking**
Secures panels at the right angle (30–45°, south-facing) for maximum sun exposure.

**Monitoring Systems**
Tracks your system's energy output and health. Accessible via mobile app or web dashboard. Check it weekly to catch any issues early.

## Key Terms

**kW (Kilowatt)** — The capacity of your system — how much power it can produce at once.

**kWh (Kilowatt-hour)** — The amount of energy produced or used over time. This is what shows on your utility bill.

**Photovoltaic effect** — The process that converts sunlight into electrical current inside a solar cell.

## Common Questions

**Do panels work on cloudy days?**
Yes, at reduced output. No output at night, but you draw from the grid when needed.

**What about winter and snow?**
Panels still work in cold weather. Snow typically slides off the smooth glass surface once the panel warms up.

**How long do systems last?**
25–30 years with minimal maintenance. Most panels carry a 25-year performance warranty.
